Facilitator: Let's walk through how your insurance company handles policies and claims.

Claims Manager: Absolutely. We start with clients -- some people call them "policyholders," it's the same thing. Each client is identified by a client number. We record their full name and date of birth.

Facilitator: How do you contact clients?

Claims Manager: Every client must have at least one contact method on file -- either a phone number or an email address, or both. We cannot have a client with neither. Some clients have both, some have just phone, some have just email.

Facilitator: Tell me about policies.

Underwriter: A policy is identified by a policy number. Each policy is issued to exactly one client, but a client can hold multiple policies. Every policy has an effective date and an expiration date.

Facilitator: What types of policies exist?

Underwriter: We have three types: auto, home, and health. A policy is always exactly one of these -- never two at once, and every policy must be one of them. Auto policies have a vehicle identification number -- we call it a VIN. Home policies have a property address. Health policies have a group number for the employer plan.

Facilitator: How does coverage work within a policy?

Underwriter: Each policy includes one or more coverages. A coverage is identified by the combination of its policy and a coverage type -- things like "liability", "collision", "comprehensive", "dwelling", or "medical". Each coverage has a coverage limit, which is a dollar amount, and a deductible. The deductible must be one of: 250, 500, 1000, 2000, or 5000 dollars.

Facilitator: Now, claims. How do those work?

Claims Manager: A claim is what happens when a client reports an incident against a coverage on their policy. We think of a claim as a thing in itself -- it's really the event of a client filing against a specific coverage. Each claim gets a claim number. A claim also has a date filed, a description of the incident, and a claimed amount.

Facilitator: What are the possible statuses for a claim?

Claims Manager: A claim goes through statuses: open, under-review, approved, denied, or settled. Every claim must have a status. A settled claim also has a settlement amount and a settlement date.

Facilitator: Who handles the claims?

Claims Manager: We have adjusters, identified by an adjuster ID. Each adjuster has a name and a license number issued by the state. An adjuster is assigned to review claims. Each claim is reviewed by exactly one adjuster, but an adjuster can handle many claims. However, an adjuster can handle at most five open claims at any given time.

Facilitator: Are there rules about which adjusters handle which claims?

Claims Manager: Yes. Each adjuster has one or more specializations -- auto, home, or health. An adjuster can only be assigned to a claim whose coverage is on a policy type matching one of the adjuster's specializations. So an adjuster who specializes in auto and home cannot handle a health claim.

Facilitator: Can a client file multiple claims?

Claims Manager: Yes, but we limit it. A client can file at most five claims per calendar year across all their policies. If they exceed that, it triggers a review.

Facilitator: Is there any hierarchy among adjusters?

Claims Manager: We have senior adjusters and junior adjusters. These are types of adjuster -- every adjuster is one or the other. Senior adjusters can approve claims up to a higher dollar threshold. Junior adjusters have a supervising senior adjuster. Every junior adjuster must have a supervisor.
