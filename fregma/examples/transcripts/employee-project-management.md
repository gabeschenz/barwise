Facilitator: Let's map out how your organization manages employees, departments, and project assignments.

HR Director: Sure. We track employees -- or "staff" as some teams call them -- by their employee number. Every employee has a name and a hire date. We also record their salary, which is a dollar amount.

Facilitator: How are departments structured?

HR Director: Each department has a department code, like "ENG" or "MKT", and a name. Every department must have a manager, and the manager must be someone who works in that department. You can't manage a department you don't belong to.

Facilitator: Can an employee work in multiple departments?

HR Director: No, each employee works in exactly one department. But a department can have many employees, of course.

Facilitator: Tell me about the management hierarchy.

HR Director: Every employee except the CEO reports to exactly one other employee. An employee cannot report to themselves -- that makes no sense. And the reporting chain has to be acyclic, meaning you can't have circular reporting where A reports to B who reports to C who reports to A.

Facilitator: Is there a mentorship program?

HR Director: Yes. Senior employees can mentor junior employees. An employee cannot mentor themselves. And if Alice mentors Bob, Bob cannot also mentor Alice -- it's a one-way relationship. A mentor can have multiple mentees, and a mentee can have at most one mentor. Mentoring is optional -- not everyone participates.

Facilitator: Now tell me about projects.

Project Manager: Projects are identified by a project code. Each project has a title and a start date. A project may or may not have an end date -- active projects don't have one yet.

Facilitator: How are employees assigned to projects?

Project Manager: An employee is assigned to a project with a specific role -- like "developer", "tester", "analyst", or "lead". We think of these assignments as things in their own right because we track the assignment date and a performance rating for each assignment. The rating is a whole number from 1 to 5.

Facilitator: Can an employee be on multiple projects?

Project Manager: Yes, but each employee can work on at least one and at most three projects at any given time. And a given employee can only hold one role per project -- you can't be both a developer and a tester on the same project.

Facilitator: Are there different types of employees?

HR Director: We distinguish between full-time and part-time employees. Every employee is either full-time or part-time, never both, and they must be one or the other. Full-time employees have a benefits tier -- gold, silver, or bronze. Part-time employees have a weekly hours cap, which is a whole number.

Facilitator: Any rules about project leads?

Project Manager: Yes. Every project must have exactly one employee assigned as "lead". And the lead of a project must work in the same department as the department that sponsors the project. Each project is sponsored by exactly one department.

Facilitator: Can you clarify how the project-department sponsorship works?

Project Manager: A department sponsors projects. A project is sponsored by exactly one department. The budget for a project comes from the sponsoring department's annual budget. Each department has an annual budget, which is a dollar amount.
